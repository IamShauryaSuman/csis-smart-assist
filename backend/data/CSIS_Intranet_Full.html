Department Development Fund (DDF) — Context & Policy

As the DDF increases in amount and the Department grows in size, guidelines are necessary on how DDF funds can (and cannot) be spent.

Sources of DDF

  • W-DDF: From WILP courses
  • C-DDF: Overhead from external projects, consulting, and APPCAIR contributions

A DDF Scrutiny Committee reviews and approves all DDF applications. This proposal is valid for one year and subject to periodic review.

02

DDF Scrutiny Committee

Composition

  • DRC members (permanent)
  • HOD-CSIS (by default)
  • One Assistant Professor (selected on rotation basis)

Responsibilities

  1. Review each conference paper application; decide support extent based on corpus available, publication venue, and nature of publication. Ensure fair distribution. [Committee sans HOD-CSIS]
  2. Final approval. [HOD-CSIS]
  3. Publish DDF balance sheet once a year to department faculty. [HOD-CSIS]
03

Research Support Guidelines

Budget cap: Max 30% of C-DDF and 30% of W-DDF for research support.

Support covers conference registration, travel, and journal over-length page charges (EPC). Support is primarily for accepted papers in the form of a "Top-up".

Note: DDF has no allocation for OA Journal page charges in line with University policy.

[TOP] Top Tier

  • Journals: SciMago Q1, IEEE and ACM Transactions (overlength page charges only); interdisciplinary society journals with impact factor >5 and in Q1. (Support withdrawn once BITS provides university-wide support.)
  • Conferences: Main Track venues in {CSRankings UNION CORE-A*}

[OTH] Other — Decided by DDF Scrutiny Committee

  • OTH-1: CORE A main track paper
  • OTH-2: CORE A* parallel track (workshop/symposium/industry — NOT included) that is running ≥5 years with steering committee of reputed scholars. Examples: ICSE-SEIP, ICSE-SEET, ICSE-MSR
  • OTH-3: CORE B main track — Non-AI subject areas only
  • OTH-4: Pen & Pencil Workshops — faculty must be an invited scholar. Selected venues:
    1. Dagstuhl
    2. Lorentz Center
    3. Banff (including Banff at CMI, Chennai)
    4. American Mathematical Society
    5. Hausdorff Research Institute for Mathematics
    6. Simons Institute at UC Berkeley
    Required: Letter of invitation + list of participating scholars with affiliations.

[OTH-Students] Student-Only Papers

DDF may extend conference registration support to papers authored only by full-time students (no faculty or industry mentor as co-author) for:

  • CORE A* workshops/symposia/industry tracks (all areas) OR CORE A workshop/symposia/industry tracks for non-AI areas
  • Venue running ≥5 years
  • Steering committee comprising reputed scholars

Conference Debriefing Policy

Anyone supported by DDF (faculty or student) must give a presentation upon returning from the conference. The individual should book the CSIS meeting room for one hour (45 min + 15 min Q&A) and invite all PhD students and faculty.

Presentation must cover

  1. What is the conference about, its focus, why is it significant?
  2. Focus area/theme of the current year
  3. Location, submission volume, acceptance rate, geo distribution, who else from India/BITS attended
  4. Workshop themes — clues to new advancements and upcoming research ideas
  5. Lessons from Keynote talks
  6. Trends/biggest takeaways from the conference
  7. Your own work — brief presentation and reception (max 15 min)
  8. One paper you found interesting (10 min)
  9. Any possible collaborations identified

FDCM Policy (First Degree Course Mentor)

TAs can be termed as FDCM (First Degree Course Mentor) under the Departmental FDCM Programme (informal TAs). The programme is managed by the Department.

Selection

Done by the Instructor-in-Charge (IC), who records: (a) IC, (b) Course, (c) Student, (d) Expected duties, (e) Performance Assessment.

Student duties cannot include evaluation.

Performance Assessment

Completed by the IC at course end. Two parts:

  • Recommendation: Honorarium+Certificate (H+C) | Certificate only (C only) | None (N)
  • Comments: Short free text explaining the recommendation

Honorarium Constraints

  • Cannot be recommended for a student already receiving payment under the Institute's FDTA programme
  • Number of FDCMs with honorarium ≤ 1:20 (student-to-FDCM ratio)
  • Honorarium per FDCM ≤ ₹2,000
  • Departmental cap per semester: ₹50,000
  • If recommendations exceed cap, number of eligible students reduced accordingly
  • Final honorarium is uniform for all FDCMs; transferred via SWD

Certificate

Does not distinguish performance categories. Contains: "Certificate of Appreciation · Awarded to: X · for excelling as a Course Mentor in subject: Y" — with department details, date, and signatures.

FDCM program uses DDF for honoraria and sundry expenses (e.g., printing certificates).

FD/HD Off-Campus Thesis Policy

Background

The department strongly recommends students collaborate with CSIS faculty for their bachelor's or master's thesis. For off-campus thesis, the department adheres to institute regulations.

Guidelines

  1. Topic must be mutually interesting to both on-campus and off-campus supervisors.
  2. Regular progress updates must be provided via presentations to the off-campus supervisor. Failure to do so = lack of progress.
  3. Thesis must address research/engineering challenges requiring thorough investigation and resolution, with robust implementation and evaluation.
  4. Content should be submission-worthy to a reputable conference or journal (Scopus indexed) — venue quality decided jointly by both supervisors.
  5. CSIS on-campus supervisor ensures compliance with all guidelines above.

Application Process

  1. Student obtains initial approval from an on-campus CSIS faculty member.
  2. Faculty emails HOD detailing merits and attaching signed approval form. Direct emails from students not entertained.
  3. Dual Degree Students: On-campus faculty must be from CSIS and the other primary department.

Exceptional Circumstances

Choosing a faculty from another department as main on-campus mentor for a core CSIS thesis requires HOD approval. Strongly discouraged and generally NOT approved.

Appendix: What Makes a Good Thesis?

  • Clear research/engineering question or objective
  • Thorough literature review
  • Well-defined methodology for data collection, analysis, and solution description
  • Clear analysis and takeaways in relation to the research question
  • Well-organized structure: intro → lit review → methodology → results → discussion → conclusion
  • Critical thinking and meaningful conclusions
  • Meaningful contribution via new insights, practical implementation, or theoretical advancement

PhD Hall Key Policy

Keys to PhD Hall Rooms D-152, D-154, D-251 are stored in the ESC Room (D-252) and must not be returned to the Security Key Room under any circumstance.

A Key Register is placed in ESC Room (D-252) to record IN/OUT entries.

Rules for PhD Scholars

  1. Mandatory Register Entry: Log date, time, name, and signature every time a key is collected or returned.
  2. No Off-Campus Carrying: Keys must remain on campus — not in hostels or homes.
  3. Responsibility for Return: Last person using the room is responsible for returning the key to D-252. Person who last signed out the key is held accountable for loss/misplacement.
  4. Access Restrictions: Only PhD Scholars officially allotted a room may use it. Others need prior approval.
  5. Security & Cleanliness: Lock rooms when unoccupied; maintain cleanliness.

For access to ESC Room: Contact Shreenivas, Neha, or Aditi. For clarifications: CSIS Office.

TA Allocation Algorithm

TA allocation is based on the principle of stable marriage using a modified form of Gale & Shapley's algorithm.

Stable Allocation Definition

No scenario where: Student s1 gets TAship for Course c1 but prefers c2, AND Course c2 has Student s2 as TA but prefers s1.

Phase 1 — Generate TA Quotas

Quota generation uses the following course attributes:

  • Course Type: CDC without lab · CDC with Lab · Elective · Elective with Lab/Assignment · FD/HD/WILP
  • Typical course strength (n): Based on historical enrollment from the previous semester

Weight hierarchy: CDC with Lab > Elective with Lab > CDC without Lab > Elective.

wavg_c = (n_c × w_c) / Σ(n_i × w_i) — then quota = Total_TA × wavg_c

Example: 4 courses, Course A is CDC with Lab (w=1.5), 120 students → n×w=180. If sum of all n×w=630, then wavg_A=0.286. With 20 FDTAs available → Course A gets ~6 FDTAs.

Phase 2 — Allocate TAs

Students submit 5 preferences (decreasing order, across FD and HD courses) via web GUI.

Course preferences for FD students are ranked by CGPA and subject grade. Ties broken randomly.

Algorithm Steps (Stable Marriage)

  1. Round 1: Students propose first preference → course provisionally accepts top-k → rejected students move to Round 2
  2. Round 2: Rejected students propose second preference → course re-ranks mixing new + provisional TAs → selects top-k → rejections proceed
  3. Algorithm terminates when no students remain to propose or all preferences exhausted

Applied separately to FDTAs, HDTAs, and PhDTAs. For HD/PhD students (no CGPA/grade data), courses make no preference → first-come-first-allocated with randomness.

Final Allocation

Not guaranteed every student gets a TAship or every course gets a TA. Output is reviewed and corner cases handled manually before submission to the instructions office.

  2. Thesis & Synopsis must follow prescribed format
  3. List of publications & references must be added in synopsis
  4. Upload to ERP: (1) Thesis (2) Synopsis (3) Two DAC member reports (4) Pre-submission presentation notice from DRC (5) Thesis submission fee receipt (6) Supervisor consent form (7) Turnitin report (8) Thesis Form I & Form II

For Supervisor

  1. Verify all documents
  2. Get examiner list approved by DRC
  3. Upload all examiner details in ERP
  4. Send signed documents of examiner list to DRC Convener

For DRC Convener

  1. Verify all documents
  2. Upload signed examiner list documents in ERP and approve

For HoD

  1. Verify all documents
  2. Approve
